WINEMAKING PROCESS
Step 1
🍇 Harvesting
All wines
Grapes are harvested by hand or machine at optimal ripeness. Balance of sugar, acidity and aroma is key.
💡 Riper grapes → more sugar → higher alcohol
Step 2
🫨 Crushing
Red
Grapes are crushed. Skins, seeds and pulp remain together — this is called must.
⚠️ Key difference from white wine: skins remain! They give colour and tannins.
Step 3
🧫 Fermentation with skins
Red
Yeast: sugar → CO₂ + alcohol. Temperature 20–32°C. Must is regularly punched down.
🌡️ Higher temperature → more colour and tannins extracted from skins
Step 4
🗜️ Pressing
Red
Liquid is separated from solids. Free-run wine (best quality) + press wine (more tannic).
Step 5
🥛 Malolactic Fermentation (MLF)
Red
Bacteria convert sharp malic acid → softer lactic acid. Wine becomes rounder. Used in almost all red wines.
Step 6
🛢️ Oak Ageing
Optional
French or American oak adds vanilla, spice, toast, cedar. Micro-oxygenation also occurs.
🇫🇷 French oak — subtle, elegant · 🇺🇸 American oak — coconut, vanilla
Steps 7–8
🧹 Fining → Bottling
All wines
Sediment removal and bottling. Closure: natural cork, synthetic cork or screwcap.
🌍 Screwcap popular in Australia & NZ — eliminates cork taint
Step 1
🍇 Harvest — slightly earlier
White
White grapes are harvested earlier than red to preserve acidity and freshness. Night harvesting helps retain aromas.
Step 2
🗜️ Immediate Pressing
White
Key difference: skins are immediately separated from juice. This prevents tannin extraction.
⚠️ This is why white wine has almost no tannins
Step 3
🧫 Low Temperature Fermentation
White
Temperature 12–22°C — lower than red wine. Low temperature preserves delicate aromas and fruitiness.
Step 4
🥛 MLF — selective use
Optional
Only for full-bodied whites (Chardonnay). Adds buttery texture. Not used for Riesling or Sauvignon Blanc.
Steps 5–6
🛢️ Ageing (steel or oak) → Bottling
All wines
Most whites in steel (preserves freshness). Chardonnay often in oak. Whites are usually drunk young.
Steps 1–4
🍾 Base Wine
Sparkling
First a still base wine is made like a white wine. High acidity and low alcohol (~10–11%).
Step 5
🧪 Tirage (adding sugar + yeast)
Sparkling
Sugar and yeast (liqueur de tirage) are added to base wine, bottled and sealed with a temporary crown cap.
Step 6
🫧 Secondary Fermentation in bottle
Sparkling
CO₂ cannot escape — it dissolves into the wine. This is how bubbles form! Bottles lie horizontal for at least 15 months.
💡 Champagne NV — minimum 15 months · Vintage — 36+ months
Step 7
🔄 Riddling (remuage)
Sparkling
Bottles are gradually tilted neck-down. Sediment collects in the neck.
Step 8
❄️ Disgorgement (dégorgement)
Sparkling
The neck is frozen, pressure ejects the frozen sediment plug. Wine remains clear!
Step 9
🍬 Dosage → Style
Sparkling
Wine + sugar mixture added. The sugar level determines the style: Brut Nature · Brut · Extra Dry · Sec · Demi-Sec · Doux
🥂 Brut (up to 12 g/L) — the most popular Champagne style

GLASSWARE & DECANTING
🔴 Full-bodied red
Wide bowl — large aeration surface. Decant 30–60 min for young wines.
🔴 Light red
Tulip-shaped glass. No decanting needed — preserves delicate aromas.
🟡 White
Smaller than red glass. Hold by the stem to keep wine cool.
🫧 Sparkling
Flute or tulip glass. Flute shows bubbles better. Tulip concentrates aromas.

🪨 SOIL TYPES AND THEIR INFLUENCE
🪨
Slate / Schist
Retains heat, excellent drainage. Poor in nutrients.
→ Mosel (Riesling), Douro (Port)
🧱
Limestone / Chalk
Good drainage, retains moisture. Promotes high acidity.
→ Champagne, Chablis (Chardonnay), Burgundy
🪵
Gravel
Excellent drainage, warms well. Vine roots deep for water.
→ Médoc (Cab Sauv), Graves (Bordeaux)
🌑
Volcanic Soil
Porous, mineral-rich. Gives minerality and smokiness.
→ Etna (Sicily), Santorini (Greece)
🟤
Clay
Retains water and heat. Suits red varieties.
→ Pomerol (Merlot), Chianti
⚪
Sand
Poor water/nutrient retention. Protects from phylloxera.
→ Colares (Portugal), parts of Bordeaux
🌿 ANNUAL VINEYARD CYCLE
Winter · Dec–Feb
✂️ Pruning
Remove last year's shoots. Determines grape cluster number — affects yield.
Spring · Mar–May
🌱 Budburst
New shoots emerge. Frost risk — can destroy entire harvest.
Summer · Jun–Aug
🌸 Flowering → Set
Flowering → pollination → berry formation. Rain during flowering = smaller yield.
Aug–Sept
🍇 Véraison — colour change
Berries change colour (red) or become translucent (white). Sugar accumulation begins.
September–October
🌾 Harvest
Optimal moment: balance of sugar and acidity. Hand or machine harvest.
Autumn · Post-harvest
🍂 Leaf fall → Dormancy
Vine stores nutrients in roots and wood. Preparing for winter.

🔍 DEEPER LOOK: LEVEL 1 VARIETIES
Variety Styles & Appellations Key L2 facts
🟡 Chardonnay Chablis (no oak) · Côte de Beaune (oak) · Champagne (sparkling) MLF gives butter/cream. No MLF = crisp and mineral.
🟡 Sauvignon Blanc Sancerre · Pouilly-Fumé (Loire) · Marlborough (NZ) · Pessac-Léognan (Bordeaux) Loire — grass/mineral. NZ — tropical. Bordeaux — complex, blended with Sémillon.
🟡 Riesling Spätlese · Auslese · Beerenauslese (Germany) · Dry Alsace German classification by grape ripeness. Petrol aroma = age, not a fault.
🔴 Cabernet Sauvignon Médoc · Pauillac · Margaux (Bordeaux) · Napa Valley · Coonawarra (Australia) In Bordeaux always blended with Merlot. New World — often varietal (100%). Decant 60+ min.
🔴 Pinot Noir Côte de Nuits (Burgundy) · Carneros (California) · Marlborough (NZ) Fussy grape — prefers cool climate. Burgundy — the most complex and expensive terroir.
🔴 Syrah / Shiraz Hermitage · Crozes-Hermitage (Rhône) · Barossa Valley (Australia) Syrah (France) — restrained, peppery. Shiraz (Australia) — jammy, opulent. Same grape — two worlds.

BubblesLarge · disappear quickly

Used forCheap sparkling wines

Food & Wine Pairing
🧠 KEY PAIRING PRINCIPLES
⚖️
Principle 1: Match Intensity
Wine weight = food weight
RuleLight food → light wine. Rich food → rich wine.
Example ✅Oysters + Muscadet · Steak + Cab Sauv
Example ❌Oysters + Barolo — wine overwhelms
💡 Ask: which is heavier — wine or dish? They should be equal so neither overwhelms.
🍋
Principle 2: Acidity is Your Friend
High acidity cuts through fat
How it worksAcidity refreshes palate · cuts richness
Examples ✅Chablis + oysters · Riesling + pork
Also works withTomato sauce + Chianti = harmony
💡 Wine must be at least as acidic as the food — otherwise wine tastes flat.
🍖
Principle 3: Tannins & Protein
Tannins softened by protein
How it worksProtein binds tannins → wine tastes softer
Examples ✅Barolo + rare steak · Cab Sauv + lamb
Avoid ❌Tannic red + fish = metallic aftertaste
Avoid ❌Tannic red + spicy = fire in mouth!
💡 Fat also softens tannins — Stilton + Port is classic for exactly this reason.
🍯
Principle 4: Sweetness & Spice
Sweetness tames heat
How it worksResidual sugar cools spice heat
Examples ✅Riesling Spätlese + Thai · Gewürz + curry
Wine must beAt least as sweet as the dish
💡 Spicy food makes wine taste drier. Off-dry whites are best. Avoid high alcohol — amplifies heat.
🧀
Principle 5: Salt & Acidity
Salt enhances wine
How it worksSalt reduces bitterness · highlights fruit
Examples ✅Sancerre + goat cheese · Fino + olives
Sweet + saltySauternes + Roquefort = classic contrast!
💡 Contrasting works too: Sauternes + Roquefort = sweetness meets saltiness.
🪞
Principle 6: Two Strategies
Congruent or Contrasting
CongruentSimilar flavours reinforce each other
Congruent exampleCream sauce + oaked Chardonnay = butter²
ContrastingOpposite flavours create tension
Contrasting exampleRich duck + acidic Pinot Noir = fat vs fresh
💡 Both work! Congruent = comfort, contrasting = excitement.

✅
What Makes Wine Taste Better
Food improves wine
Salty food→ reduces bitterness · enhances fruit
Acidic food→ wine tastes sweeter and softer
Umami in food→ enhances wine body and flavour
Fat in food→ softens tannins · rounder

Food worsens wine
Sweet food→ wine tastes drier, bitter, sour
Spicy food→ amplifies alcohol · tannins burn
Bitter food→ amplifies wine bitterness
Vinegar / Citrus→ kills wine aromas · tastes flat

⚠️ COMMON PAIRING MISTAKES
🐟
Red wine + fish
Very common mistake
ProblemTannins + fish oils = metallic aftertaste
SolutionWhite / rosé · or light Pinot Noir
💡 Exception: fatty tuna + light Pinot Noir works! Barolo + sole = disaster.
🌶️
Red wine + spicy food
Fire in your mouth
ProblemAlcohol + spice = fire · tannins = bitter
SolutionOff-dry white · low alcohol
💡 Grenache/Zinfandel (high ABV) = worst with spice. Riesling Spätlese = best.
🎂
Dry wine + sweet dessert
Wine tastes sour and bitter
ProblemSweet food makes dry wine taste sour
SolutionWine ≥ sweet as dessert
💡 Brut Champagne + chocolate cake = disappointment. Use Recioto or Tawny Port instead.
🥗
Vinaigrette + wine
Wine's worst enemy at the table
ProblemVinegar kills wine aromas
SolutionLemon dressing instead · or very acidic wine
💡 Serve wine with salad? Use lemon juice not vinegar in the dressing.
🧄
Artichoke + wine
Makes everything taste sweet
ProblemCynarin makes wine taste sweet
SolutionDry neutral white · or water!
💡 Artichoke is notoriously difficult with wine. Verdicchio or Vernaccia are among the few that work.
🍳
Eggs + tannic red
Sulphur vs tannins
ProblemEgg sulphur + tannins = metallic
SolutionChampagne · Chablis · light Pinot Noir
💡 Eggs Benedict + Champagne is classic brunch for a reason.
