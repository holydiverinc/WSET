WINEMAKING PROCESS
Step 1
🍇 Harvesting
All wines
Grapes are harvested by hand or machine at optimal ripeness. Balance of sugar, acidity and aroma is key.
💡 Riper grapes → more sugar → higher alcohol
Step 2
🫨 Crushing
Red
Grapes are crushed. Skins, seeds and pulp remain together — this is called must.
⚠️ Key difference from white wine: skins remain! They give colour and tannins.
Step 3
🧫 Fermentation with skins
Red
Yeast: sugar → CO₂ + alcohol. Temperature 20–32°C. Must is regularly punched down.
🌡️ Higher temperature → more colour and tannins extracted from skins
Step 4
🗜️ Pressing
Red
Liquid is separated from solids. Free-run wine (best quality) + press wine (more tannic).
Step 5
🥛 Malolactic Fermentation (MLF)
Red
Bacteria convert sharp malic acid → softer lactic acid. Wine becomes rounder. Used in almost all red wines.
Step 6
🛢️ Oak Ageing
Optional
French or American oak adds vanilla, spice, toast, cedar. Micro-oxygenation also occurs.
🇫🇷 French oak — subtle, elegant · 🇺🇸 American oak — coconut, vanilla
Steps 7–8
🧹 Fining → Bottling
All wines
Sediment removal and bottling. Closure: natural cork, synthetic cork or screwcap.
🌍 Screwcap popular in Australia & NZ — eliminates cork taint
Step 1
🍇 Harvest — slightly earlier
White
White grapes are harvested earlier than red to preserve acidity and freshness. Night harvesting helps retain aromas.
Step 2
🗜️ Immediate Pressing
White
Key difference: skins are immediately separated from juice. This prevents tannin extraction.
⚠️ This is why white wine has almost no tannins
Step 3
🧫 Low Temperature Fermentation
White
Temperature 12–22°C — lower than red wine. Low temperature preserves delicate aromas and fruitiness.
Step 4
🥛 MLF — selective use
Optional
Only for full-bodied whites (Chardonnay). Adds buttery texture. Not used for Riesling or Sauvignon Blanc.
Steps 5–6
🛢️ Ageing (steel or oak) → Bottling
All wines
Most whites in steel (preserves freshness). Chardonnay often in oak. Whites are usually drunk young.
Steps 1–4
🍾 Base Wine
Sparkling
First a still base wine is made like a white wine. High acidity and low alcohol (~10–11%).
Step 5
🧪 Tirage (adding sugar + yeast)
Sparkling
Sugar and yeast (liqueur de tirage) are added to base wine, bottled and sealed with a temporary crown cap.
Step 6
🫧 Secondary Fermentation in bottle
Sparkling
CO₂ cannot escape — it dissolves into the wine. This is how bubbles form! Bottles lie horizontal for at least 15 months.
💡 Champagne NV — minimum 15 months · Vintage — 36+ months
Step 7
🔄 Riddling (remuage)
Sparkling
Bottles are gradually tilted neck-down. Sediment collects in the neck.
Step 8
❄️ Disgorgement (dégorgement)
Sparkling
The neck is frozen, pressure ejects the frozen sediment plug. Wine remains clear!
Step 9
🍬 Dosage → Style
Sparkling
Wine + sugar mixture added. The sugar level determines the style: Brut Nature · Brut · Extra Dry · Sec · Demi-Sec · Doux
🥂 Brut (up to 12 g/L) — the most popular Champagne style

GLASSWARE & DECANTING
🔴 Full-bodied red
Wide bowl — large aeration surface. Decant 30–60 min for young wines.
🔴 Light red
Tulip-shaped glass. No decanting needed — preserves delicate aromas.
🟡 White
Smaller than red glass. Hold by the stem to keep wine cool.
🫧 Sparkling
Flute or tulip glass. Flute shows bubbles better. Tulip concentrates aromas.

🪨 SOIL TYPES AND THEIR INFLUENCE
🪨
Slate / Schist
Retains heat, excellent drainage. Poor in nutrients.
→ Mosel (Riesling), Douro (Port)
🧱
Limestone / Chalk
Good drainage, retains moisture. Promotes high acidity.
→ Champagne, Chablis (Chardonnay), Burgundy
🪵
Gravel
Excellent drainage, warms well. Vine roots deep for water.
→ Médoc (Cab Sauv), Graves (Bordeaux)
🌑
Volcanic Soil
Porous, mineral-rich. Gives minerality and smokiness.
→ Etna (Sicily), Santorini (Greece)
🟤
Clay
Retains water and heat. Suits red varieties.
→ Pomerol (Merlot), Chianti
⚪
Sand
Poor water/nutrient retention. Protects from phylloxera.
→ Colares (Portugal), parts of Bordeaux
🌿 ANNUAL VINEYARD CYCLE
Winter · Dec–Feb
✂️ Pruning
Remove last year's shoots. Determines grape cluster number — affects yield.
Spring · Mar–May
🌱 Budburst
New shoots emerge. Frost risk — can destroy entire harvest.
Summer · Jun–Aug
🌸 Flowering → Set
Flowering → pollination → berry formation. Rain during flowering = smaller yield.
Aug–Sept
🍇 Véraison — colour change
Berries change colour (red) or become translucent (white). Sugar accumulation begins.
September–October
🌾 Harvest
Optimal moment: balance of sugar and acidity. Hand or machine harvest.
Autumn · Post-harvest
🍂 Leaf fall → Dormancy
Vine stores nutrients in roots and wood. Preparing for winter.

Variety Styles & Appellations Key L2 facts
🟡 Chardonnay Chablis (no oak) · Côte de Beaune (oak) · Champagne (sparkling) MLF gives butter/cream. No MLF = crisp and mineral.

🔴 Syrah / Shiraz Hermitage · Crozes-Hermitage (Rhône) · Barossa Valley (Australia) Syrah (France) — restrained, peppery. Shiraz (Australia) — jammy, opulent. Same grape — two worlds.
